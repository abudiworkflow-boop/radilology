#### Terminology

Tibial plateau fractures were originally termed **bump**, **bumper,** or **fender fractures**, but other mechanisms (e.g. falls) were subsequently identified 5.

#### Epidemiology

Tibial plateau fractures account for 1-2% of all adult fractures 4,5. Men are more commonly affected than women (M:F = 3:1) 8.

#### Mechanism

The most common mechanism of injury involves axial loading, e.g. a fall from a significant height. In younger patients, the most common pattern of fracture is splitting, while in older, more osteoporotic patients, depression fractures are typically sustained.

Fractures of the lateral plateau are much more common than those of the medial plateau, as a larger force is required for the latter.

##### Associations

Associated injuries are common:

- ligament injury (~25%; range 20-30%) 8

- meniscal injury (~30%; range 10-47%) 8

- fibular head fractures are associated with medial tibial plateau fractures 5

- posterior tibial / common peroneal nerve injury (~3%; range 2-4%) 8

#### Classification

The three most commonly used classification systems (c. 2024) 4:

- [Schatzker classification](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/schatzker-classification-of-tibial-plateau-fractures-1?lang=us "Schatzker classification of tibial plateau fractures")

- OTA/AO classification

- Hohl classification

Other classification systems include:

- [Kfuri and Schatzker classification](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/kfuri-and-schatzker-classification-of-tibial-plateau-fractures-1?lang=us "Kfuri and Schatzker classification of tibial plateau fractures")

- 3-column classification by Luo et al. 4

#### Radiographic features

Tibial plateau fractures are complex injuries that require adequate imaging to assess prior to fixation.

##### Plain radiograph

Plain radiography often underestimates the severity of the injury. [Lipohemarthrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/lipohaemarthrosis?lang=us) should be present.

##### CT

CT is very helpful in accurately defining the extent of the bony injury and facilitates orthopedic intervention.

##### MRI

MRI is very helpful in the assessment of soft tissue injury around the joint.

#### Treatment and prognosis

The goal of therapy is to reduce the fracture and begin early mobilization. [ORIF](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/open-reduction-internal-fixation?lang=us "ORIF (orthopaedic surgery)") is the current (c. 2023) gold standard 7. If the patient is immobilized for a lengthy period (>3 weeks), the joint will not return to the full range of movement.

Depression of a tibial plateau that is inadequately corrected results in a [varus](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=varus-deformity&lang=us) or [valgus deformity](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=valgus-deformity&lang=us) and accelerated [osteoarthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteoarthritis?lang=us).

Unappreciated ligamentous injury causes greater than normal stress on the remaining support structures of the joint, malalignment, and the development of premature osteoarthritis.

##### Complications

- infection (~10%; range 6.4-16.1%) 8

- [deep vein thrombosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/deep-vein-thrombosis?lang=us "Deep vein thrombosis") (~4%; range 1.8-5.6%) 8

- [post-traumatic osteoarthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/post-traumatic-osteoarthritis?lang=us "Post-traumatic osteoarthritis") (~40%; range 24-73.3%) 8
  - ~5% (range 0-21%) of patients will go onto [total knee arthroplasty](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/total-knee-arthroplasty?lang=us "Total knee arthroplasty") 7
