**Intracranial aneurysms**, also called **cerebral aneurysms**, are [aneurysms](/articles/aneurysm?lang=us) of the [intracranial arteries](/articles/intracranial-arteries?lang=us). The most common morphologic type is the [saccular aneurysm](/articles/saccular-cerebral-aneurysm?lang=us), although a number of other morphologies and etiologies occur. 

Due to a combination of thinner/weaker walls and [Laplace's law](/articles/laplaces-law?lang=us), aneurysms tend to enlarge progressively. This, in turn, can lead to rupture (and subarachnoid hemorrhage) or partial thrombosis (due to slow turbulent flow). 

#### Pathology

There is not a universal classification for the types of intracranial aneurysms, resulting in a heterogeneous mix of terms based on the morphology, size, location, and etiology 1,2:

[saccular intracranial aneurysm](/articles/saccular-cerebral-aneurysm?lang=us)

[giant intracranial aneurysm](/articles/giant-cerebral-aneurysm?lang=us)

- [fusiform intracranial aneurysm](/articles/fusiform-intracranial-aneurysm?lang=us)

- [blood blister-like aneurysm](/articles/blood-blister-like-aneurysm?lang=us)

- [dissecting intracranial aneurysm](/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=dissecting-intracranial-aneurysm&lang=us)

- [mycotic (infectious) intracranial aneurysm](/articles/intracranial-mycotic-aneurysm?lang=us)

- [traumatic intracranial aneurysm](/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=traumatic-intracranial-aneurysm&lang=us)

- [neoplastic (oncotic) intracranial aneurysm](/articles/neoplastic-intracranial-aneurysm?lang=us)
