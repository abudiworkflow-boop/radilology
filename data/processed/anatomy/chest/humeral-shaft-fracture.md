#### Epidemiology

Humeral shaft fractures account for 3-5% of all fractures 1,3. Although they occur in all age groups, a bimodal distribution is noted. The first peak is seen in the third decade in males and the second peak in the seventh decade in females 3.

#### Pathology

##### Mechanism

Most frequently humeral shaft fractures occur as a result of a direct blow to the upper arm (transverse fractures). Indirect trauma from a fall or a twisting action (e.g. arm wrestling) is also encountered and usually results in spiral or oblique fractures 1,3. The higher the impact strength, the more likely the fracture is to be comminuted 1. A minority are [open fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/open-fracture?lang=us) (2-10%) 3.

The most common associated injury is damage to the radial nerve (particularly with [Holstein-Lewis fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/holstein-lewis-fracture?lang=us "Holstein-Lewis fractures")), on account of its very close relationship to the posterior aspect of the bone, running in the spiral groove between the lateral and medial heads of the triceps muscle 1.

Damage to the [brachial artery](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/brachial-artery?lang=us), [median](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/median-nerve-2?lang=us) and [ulnar nerves](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/ulnar-nerve?lang=us) are much less common.

#### Radiographic features

Fractures are unevenly distributed along the humeral diaphysis 3:

- proximal third: 30%
- middle third: 60%
- distal third: 10%

##### Plain radiograph

Although transverse fractures are usually trivially easy to identify, oblique or spiral fractures can be very difficult to identify in a single view. The radiographic series comprises an [AP](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/humerus-ap-view-3?lang=us) and a [lateral](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/humerus-lateral-view-3?lang=us) projection to allow adequate assessment of angulation, displacement and shortening 2,3. It is essential both the shoulder joint and the elbow joint are included to assess for proximal or distal extension of the fracture.

###### Report checklist

In addition to reporting on the presence of a fracture, a number of features should be assessed and commented on:

- fracture
  - location and extension to metaphysis/epiphysis/articular surface
  - type of fracture ( [transverse](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/transverse-fracture?lang=us), [spiral](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/spiral-fracture?lang=us), [oblique](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/oblique-fracture?lang=us))
  - [comminution](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/comminuted-fracture?lang=us)/ [segmental](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/segmental-fracture-1?lang=us)
  - [angulation](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/fracture-angulation?lang=us), [displacement](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/fracture-displacement-summary?lang=us) and shortening
- [open](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/open-fracture?lang=us) vs. closed; gas in soft tissues or foreign bodies
- underlying bony lesions (i.e. [pathological fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pathological-fracture?lang=us))
- carefully assess the elbow and shoulder for secondary injuries (be careful, as these will be sub-optimally imaged unless dedicated views are obtained)

#### Treatment and prognosis

Humeral shaft fractures are usually treated with a supportive/hanging cast followed by a supportive splint (e.g. coaptation splinting such as a Sarmiento brace) and infrequently require open reduction. Although anatomical reduction is not easily achieved, significant angulation (20 degrees) can be tolerated with little functional impairment. Similarly, up to 3-5 cm of shortening is in many cases acceptable 1-3.

Although there are no differences in functional outcomes between operative and non-operative treatment, the most common reason for converting non-operative treatment to operative treatment is non-union (incidence up to 33%) 6. Union rates range from 0-13% for patients undergoing surgery 6. Recovery with surgery also allows for a quicker and most reliable recovery 6.

Open reduction - internal fixation ( [ORIF](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/open-reduction-internal-fixation?lang=us)) is required in a number of scenarios, including 1,2:

- adequate alignment cannot be maintained (more commonly encountered in transverse fractures)
- open fractures
- presence of vascular injury
- [segmental fracture](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/segmental-fracture-1?lang=us)
- [floating elbow](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=floating-elbow&lang=us)
- presence of significant other injuries (poly-trauma, brachial plexus injury)
- non-union
- [pathological fracture](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pathological-fracture?lang=us)

In many instances presence of [radial nerve](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/radial-nerve-2?lang=us) palsy is not an indication for exploration, with the majority of patients recovering radial nerve function within 3-6 months 1. The role of open reduction and internal fixation in this situation is controversial 1,2.
