**Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)** is a chronic autoimmune multisystemic inflammatory disease that affects many organs but predominantly attacks the synovial tissues and joints.

The pediatric condition, [juvenile rheumatoid arthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/juvenile-rheumatoid-arthritis?lang=us), is discussed separately.

#### Epidemiology

The overall prevalence is 0.5-1%, and the disease is 2-3 times more common in women 1. Onset is generally in adulthood, with peak incidence typically occurring between ages 30 and 50 years (fourth and fifth decades), although the disease demonstrates age-related incidence with substantial numbers of cases presenting after age 60 16. The mean age at diagnosis varies across populations but is commonly reported around 55 years 16,17.

##### Risk factors

- [smoking](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tobacco-use-1?lang=us "Smoking") (seropositive rheumatoid arthritis) 12

- oral/respiratory infection 15

- [dust inhalation](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pneumoconiosis?lang=us "Pneumoconiosis") (silica/air pollution) 15

- diet and lifestyle 15

- low socioeconomic status 15

- [obesity](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/obesity?lang=us "Obesity") 15

- allergic respiratory disease ( [bronchial asthma](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/asthma-1?lang=us "Bronchial asthma") / [COPD](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease-1?lang=us "COPD (lung)")) 15

- immune-mediated diseases ( [inflammatory bowel disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/inflammatory-bowel-disease?lang=us "Inflammatory bowel disease"), [Graves disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/graves-disease?lang=us "Graves disease")) 15

- genetic/epigenetic causes 15

#### Diagnosis

##### Diagnostic criteria

Diagnosis is based on a combination of clinical, radiographic, and serological criteria. The 2010 ACR-EULAR classification criteria for Rheumatoid Arthritis 4 has a maximal score of 10 and requires a score of >6 for a diagnosis of rheumatoid arthritis to be made:

- joint involvement
  - 0: 1 large joint

  - 1: 2-10 large joints

  - 2: 1-3 small joints (with or without the involvement of large joints)

  - 3: 4-10 small joints (with or without the involvement of large joints)

  - 5: >10 joints (at least 1 small joint)
- serology
  - 0: negative RF and negative anti-CCP

  - 2: low-positive RF or low-positive anti-CCP

  - 3: high-positive RF or high-positive anti-CCP
- acute phase reactants
  - 0: normal [CRP](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/c-reactive-protein-1?lang=us) and normal [ESR](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/erythrocyte-sedimentation-rate?lang=us)

  - 1: abnormal CRP and abnormal ESR
- duration of symptoms
  - 0: <6 weeks

  - 1: >6 weeks

#### Clinical presentation

The clinical features can be broadly categorized as articular and extra-articular. Generally, the onset of the condition may be insidious or abrupt, and early features commonly include tiredness, malaise, and generalized aches ref. Articular features generally develop before extra-articular features ref.

Articular features include a symmetrical deforming peripheral [polyarthropathy](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/arthritis?lang=us "Arthritis") that classically affects the fingers and hands, but also commonly involves the feet and can involve multiple other joints. The arthropathy has a classic inflammatory phenotype, with joint stiffness, reduced range of motion, reduced functionality, and a "boggy" feel to palpation. For further details, see [musculoskeletal manifestations of rheumatoid arthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-musculoskeletal-manifestations-2?lang=us).

Extra-articular features include ref:

- [pulmonary involvement](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-pulmonary-manifestations-1?lang=us)
  - [interstitial lung disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-associated-interstitial-lung-disease?lang=us "Rheumatoid arthritis associated interstitial lung disease")

  - [pulmonary nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-pulmonary-nodule-1?lang=us "Rheumatoid lung nodules")

  - [pleural effusion](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pleural-effusion?lang=us "Pleural effusion"), including [pseudochylothorax](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pseudochylothorax?lang=us "Pseudochylothorax")

  - [Caplan syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/caplan-syndrome?lang=us) 7: rheumatoid arthritis and pneumoconiosis
- cardiovascular involvement
  - accelerated/premature coronary artery [atherosclerosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/arteriosclerosis?lang=us "Atherosclerosis - general") 8, contributing significantly to the excess mortality of rheumatoid arthritis

  - [pericarditis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pericarditis?lang=us)
- cutaneous involvement
  - [rheumatoid nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-nodule?lang=us): usually seen in pressure areas (e.g. elbows, occiput, lumbosacral) 3 in RF-positive patients 9

  - rheumatoid [vasculitis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/vasculitis?lang=us "Vasculitis"): classically, deep cutaneous ulcers in the lower limbs may have associated nailfold lesions
- hematological involvement
  - anemia of chronic disease

  - [Felty syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/felty-syndrome?lang=us): a syndrome characterized by the triad of rheumatoid arthritis, [splenomegaly](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/splenomegaly?lang=us "Splenomegaly"), and neutropenia

  - [large granular lymphocyte leukemia](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=large-granular-lymphocyte-leukaemia&lang=us)
- ocular involvement
  - [keratoconjunctivitis sicca](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=keratoconjunctivitis-sicca&lang=us)

  - [episcleritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/episcleritis?lang=us)
- neurological involvement
  - [rheumatoid meningitis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-meningitis?lang=us "rheumatoid meningitis")

#### Pathology

##### Etiology

Etiology is unknown but is probably multifactorial. It is generally considered that a genetic predisposition (e.g. HLA-DR B1, which is the most common allele of HLA-DR4 involved in rheumatoid arthritis) and an environmental trigger (e.g. [Epstein-Barr virus](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/epstein-barr-virus-associated-diseases?lang=us "Epstein-Barr virus associated diseases"), postulated as a possible antigen, but not proven) lead to an autoimmune response that is directed against synovial structures and other organs ref.

Activation and accumulation of CD4 T cells in the synovium start a cascade of inflammatory responses, which result in 20,21:

- activation of the macrophages and synovial cells and production of cytokines such as IL4 and TNF, which in turn cause proliferation of the synovial cells and increase the production of destructive enzymes such as elastase and collagenase by macrophages

- activating B cell lymphocytes to produce various antibodies, including [rheumatoid factor](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-factor?lang=us) (RF-IgM antibodies against the Fc portion of the IgG), which makes immune complexes that deposit in different tissues and contribute to further injury

- directly activate endothelial cells via increased production of VCAM1, which increases the adhesion and accumulation of inflammatory cells

- producing RANKL, which in turn activate osteoclasts, causing subchondral bone destruction

The inflammatory response leads to [pannus](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pannus?lang=us) formation. Pannus is an edematous, thickened, hyperplastic synovium infiltrated by lymphocytes T and B, plasmocytes, macrophages, and osteoclasts. Pannus will gradually erode bare areas initially, followed by the articular cartilage. It causes fibrous ankylosis, which eventually ossifies 2,3.

##### Markers

There are several serological markers for rheumatoid arthritis ref:

- [rheumatoid factor (RF)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-factor?lang=us)
  - IgM antibody against the Fc portion of the IgG antibodies

  - traditional but non-specific marker, associated with several autoimmune and chronic infectious diseases
- anti-cyclic citrullinated peptide (anti-CCP) / anti-citrullinated plasma antibody (ACPA)
  - >80% sensitive and >95% specific
- elevated CRP and/or ESR

##### Genetics

Associated antigen subtypes include 14:

- HLA-DR4: particularly HLA-DR B1 - the most common allele of HLA-DR4

- HLA-B54

- HLA-DQB1\*0601

- HLA-B40

#### Radiographic features

- [musculoskeletal manifestations](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-musculoskeletal-manifestations-2?lang=us "Musculoskeletal manifestations of RA") are the earliest and dominant features of rheumatoid arthritis ref

- [pulmonary manifestations](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-pulmonary-manifestations-1?lang=us "Pulmonary manifestations of rheumatoid arthritis") may involve the airway, interstitium, and/or pleura, including [Caplan syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/caplan-syndrome?lang=us "Caplan syndrome")

- [cardiac manifestations](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis-cardiac-manifestations?lang=us "Rheumatoid arthritis (cardiac manifestations)") may be present in more severe cases

- abdominal manifestations include [splenomegaly](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/splenomegaly?lang=us) as part of [Felty syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/felty-syndrome?lang=us), usually in long-standing rheumatoid arthritis ref.

- neurological manifestations such as [rheumatoid meningitis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-meningitis?lang=us "Rheumatoid meningitis") are rare

#### Treatment and prognosis

Treatment of rheumatoid arthritis is aimed at improving the symptoms and slowing disease progression. Medications include:

- [non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/non-steroidal-anti-inflammatory-drugs?lang=us)

- disease-modifying anti-rheumatic drugs (DMARDs)
  - conventional synthetic DMARDs (csDMARDs): e.g. methotrexate, leflunomide, prednisolone

  - biological DMARDs (bDMARDs): e.g. TNF-α inhibitors (e.g. infliximab), tocilizumab, abatacept, rituxumab

  - targeted synthetic DMARDs (tsDMARDs): e.g. tofacitinib

The disease carries a significant burden of disability. There is also a reduction in life expectancy, with excess mortality usually related to its non-articular manifestations 5,6.

#### History and etymology

Rheumatoid arthritis was a term first coined by the British physician Sir Alfred Baring Garrod (1819-1907) in 1859 11.

#### Differential diagnosis

- [psoriatic arthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/psoriatic-arthritis?lang=us): distal distribution, new bone formation, enthesitis 12

- [erosive osteoarthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/erosive-osteoarthritis?lang=us): distal distribution, central erosions, and "gull wing" appearance 18

- [systemic lupus erythematosus](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/systemic-lupus-erythematosus?lang=us): non-erosive metacarpophalangeal joint subluxation 19

- [scleroderma](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/scleroderma-musculoskeletal-manifestations-1?lang=us "Scleroderma (musculoskeletal manifestations)") 13
  - can have similar erosions and be CCP-positive

  - soft tissue calcifications and [acro-osteolysis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/acro-osteolysis-1?lang=us "Acro-osteolysis") are characteristic findings
- [robust rheumatoid arthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/robust-rheumatoid-arthritis?lang=us) ref
