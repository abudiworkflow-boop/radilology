**Pulmonary nodules** are small, rounded opacities within the [pulmonary interstitium](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-interstitium?lang=us). Pulmonary nodules are common, and as the spatial resolution of CT scanners has increased, the detection of smaller and smaller nodules has occurred, which are more often an incidental finding (see [incidental lung nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/incidental-lung-nodules-1?lang=us "Incidental lung nodules")).

#### Pathology

Pulmonary nodules can be described according to size, morphology and/or distribution.

Acinar nodules are multiple, poorly defined, centrilobular round or oval opacities measuring 5 to 8 mm in diameter, possibly due to consolidation within an acinus 11.

A [pseudonodule](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=pseudonodule&lang=us "pseudonodule") could be caused by callous formation after a rib fracture, skin lesion, external device on the skin of the patient, anatomical variant, or overlapping areas of increased density that resemble a pulmonary nodule 11.

##### Size

- [miliary nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/miliary-opacities-lungs?lang=us): <2 mm

- [pulmonary micronodule](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-micronodule?lang=us): <6 mm (per Fleischner Society definition 11)

- [pulmonary nodule](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-nodule-1?lang=us): 6-30 mm

- [pulmonary mass](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-mass?lang=us): >30 mm

#### Radiographic features

They are generally homogeneous (without [air bronchograms](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/air-bronchograms?lang=us) or [alveolograms](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=air-alveolograms&lang=us)) and are well-defined since their margins are sharp and they are surrounded by normally aerated lung parenchyma. They are quite separate from [airspace nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/airspace-nodules?lang=us) that often have an irregular margin and are usually ~8 mm in diameter. (For further discussion, see the article on [nodular opacification](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/nodular-opacification?lang=us).)

#### Differential diagnosis

The differential diagnosis for a nodule can be refined by its size, location, and density. [Solitary pulmonary nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/solitary-pulmonary-nodule-1?lang=us) and [hyperdense pulmonary nodules](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/hyperdense-pulmonary-nodules?lang=us) are discussed further elsewhere.

A [micronodular or miliary pattern](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-micronodule?lang=us "Micronodular pattern") is predominately seen in [granulomatous processes](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/granulomatous-lung-diseases?lang=us), [hematogenous pulmonary metastases](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/lung-metastasis?lang=us), and [pneumoconioses](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pneumoconioses?lang=us). Nodules and masses are most often seen in [metastatic disease to the lung](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-metastases?lang=us).

Always be aware of artifacts, especially on radiographs, where buttons or [nipple shadows](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/nipple-shadows?lang=us) can often be mistaken for a true pulmonary nodule 7.
