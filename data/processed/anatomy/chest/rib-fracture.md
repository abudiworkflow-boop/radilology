#### Pathology

The 4th-10th ribs are the most commonly fractured 1. Fractures of the 1st-3rd ribs are associated with high-energy trauma 3.

When the rib is fractured twice, the term floating rib is used to describe the free fracture fragment, and when three or more contiguous floating ribs are present, this is called a [flail chest](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/flail-chest?lang=us).

[Buckle rib fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/buckle-rib-fracture?lang=us) can also occur, one of the rare instances of [buckle fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/torus-fracture-1?lang=us) in a mature skeleton.

##### Etiology

- blunt and penetrating trauma: e.g. motor vehicle accidents, falls, assaults
  - most common injury in blunt thoracic trauma, occurring in 50% of cases 3
- [pathological fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pathological-fractures?lang=us)

- [stress fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/stress-fracture-2?lang=us) 21
  - [​](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/stress-fracture-2?lang=us) occur more commonly in high-level athletes

  - occur laterally and anterolaterally

  - repetitive overhead movements (e.g. weight lifting, basketball) can result in 1st rib stress fractures

  - twisting movements (e.g. rowing, swimming) can result in middle and lower rib stress fractures
- [osteoporosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteoporosis-3?lang=us "Osteoporosis")
  - [decreased bone density](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteopenia?lang=us "Osteopenia") increases the risk of rib fractures 22,23
- [non-accidental injuries](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/suspected-physical-abuse-1?lang=us) in children
  - typically posterior fractures
- [intimate partner violence](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/intimate-partner-violence?lang=us)
  - typically posterior 20
- cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR): reported incidence varying from approximately 25% on clinical examination to 55% on imaging studies and up to 77-96% at autopsy 22

- [fetal rib fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/fetal-rib-fractures?lang=us): caused by skeletal dysplasias

- [radiation-induced rib fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/radiation-induced-rib-fracture-2?lang=us) 8,9

- spontaneous: [spontaneous rib fracture](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/spontaneous-rib-fracture?lang=us)

##### Associations

Rib fractures are often associated with other injuries, and the greater the number of rib fractures, the more likely are associated injuries 1,3:

- 1st \- 3rd rib fractures 25:
  - [brachial plexus](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/brachial-plexus-injuries?lang=us)

  - subclavian and neck vessel injuries

  - head injury

  - cardiac injury

  - severe thoracoabdominal trauma
- [pneumothorax](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pneumothorax?lang=us)/ [hemothorax](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/hemothorax-1?lang=us)

- [pulmonary laceration](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-laceration-1?lang=us)

- [lung herniation](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/lung-hernia-1?lang=us)

- [liver](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/liver-trauma?lang=us), [kidney](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/renal-trauma-1?lang=us) and [spleen traumatic injuries](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/splenic-trauma?lang=us) (10th-12th rib fractures)

#### Radiographic features

##### Plain radiograph

- may miss up to 50% of rib fractures even with dedicated [oblique rib projections](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=oblique-rib-radiograph&lang=us) 1

##### Ultrasound

A common application of point-of-care ultrasonography is used in a complementary manner to conventional radiography in the workup of blunt chest wall trauma and localized chest pain. Ultrasonography is more sensitive and specific than conventional radiography for rib fracture detection in blunt trauma when performed by a trained clinician 12,14.

Moreover, the use of sonography allows the detection of radiographically occult costal cartilage fractures, as well as assessment of the underlying [lung](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bedside-lung-ultrasound-in-emergency-approach?lang=us) for associated traumatic pathology, including;

- [pneumothorax](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pneumothorax-ultrasound-1?lang=us)

- [lung contusion](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-contusion?lang=us)

###### B-mode

- the cardinal sonographic feature is a discontinuity of the otherwise smooth, linear echogenicity corresponding to the anterior bony cortex

- if accompanied by cortical displacement, it may be further characterized by the axial distance between the cortical margins 14
  - ​separation by less than 1 mm defines mild displacement

  - considered severe when >4 mm
    - often associated with [comet tail](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/comet-tail-artifact-4?lang=us) artifacts extending from the fracture site into the far-field
- if initially occult, an obvious interruption of the bony cortex may be elicited with sonopalpation (applying local transducer pressure​)
  - appropriate analgesia may be necessary before attempting this maneuver
- suggestive findings include a circumscribed, [hypoechoic](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/physical-principles-of-ultrasound-1?lang=us) fluid collection adjacent to the putative fracture site
  - represents a [fracture hematoma](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/hematoma-1?lang=us), fluid nature may be ascertained through careful adjustment of gain settings and dynamic variation of the observed shape with pressure

  - associated periosteal elevation appears as a delicate, raised hyperechoic linear stripe

##### CT

- more sensitive than plain radiography for the detection of rib fractures 1,3

- displacement on CT may be defined as greater than half a rib shaft width 19

##### Nuclear medicine

- [Tc-99m bone scan](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bone-scintigraphy-1?lang=us) is sensitive but not specific for rib fractures and demonstrates focal areas of high-uptake, which need to be correlated with SPECT or radiographic imaging 1,7

#### Treatment and prognosis

Rib fractures themselves are treated symptomatically and have a good prognostic outcome. Rarely, severe rib injuries (e.g. flail chest) may be treated with [ORIF](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/open-reduction-internal-fixation?lang=us) (surgical stabilization of rib fracture), often in the setting of other severe traumatic injuries and in the hope that respiratory function will improve, facilitating a shorter ICU stay and quicker recovery.

##### Complications

Aside from immediate traumatic complications outlined above, [atelectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/segmental-atelectasis?lang=us) and [pneumonia](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/lobar-pneumonia?lang=us) may develop, mainly due to poor respiratory effort secondary to pain, and this increases the morbidity and mortality due to rib fractures 3.
