**Bronchiectasis** is irreversible dilatation of the [bronchial tree](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tracheobronchial-tree?lang=us), and is commonly accompanied by bronchial wall thickening and mucoid impaction 25.

#### Terminology

If reversibility is uncertain use the term “bronchial dilatation” 25.

The term [bronchiolectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bronchiolectasis-1?lang=us "Bronchiolectasis") refers to irreversible dilatation of distal non-cartilaginous airways.

Traction bronchiectasis refers to bronchial dilatation caused by fibrosis and is associated with irregular wall thickening 25.

Traction bronchiolectasis refers to dilatation of the non-cartilaginous airways in the central [secondary pulmonary lobule](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/secondary-pulmonary-lobule?lang=us "Secondary pulmonary lobule") which normally measure <2 mm and is typically associated with honeycombing and caused by [fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-fibrosis?lang=us "Fibrosis of lung") 25.

The plural of bronchiectasis is “bronchiectases” however this term is rarely used.

#### Epidemiology

The incidence of bronchiectasis is difficult to gauge; infection is the most common cause and the disease is more common in low socioeconomic groups. Globally, infectious diseases account for about 30% of cases and in a similar proportion the cause is unknown. Tuberculosis accounts for about 14% and [cystic fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cystic-fibrosis?lang=us "Cystic fibrosis"), [chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease-1?lang=us "Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)") and [asthma](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/asthma-1?lang=us "Asthma") make a minor contribution 26.

#### Diagnosis

Bronchial dilatation is present if the bronchial diameter exceeds the diameter of the accompanying pulmonary artery on CT ( [signet ring sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/signet-ring-disambiguation?lang=us "signet ring sign")). Absence of normal tapering and abnormal visualization of peripheral airways within 1 cm of the pleural surface are common signs of bronchiectasis 25. Thickened bronchial walls and adjacent scarring are common. Irreversibility must be established to distinguish bronchiectasis from bronchial dilatation.

#### Clinical presentation

Bronchiectasis can present with recurrent chest infections, chronic or recurrent productive cough, hemoptysis or [cor pulmonale](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cor-pulmonale-2?lang=us "Cor pulmonale").

#### Pathology

Dilatation is caused by congenital deficiency or acquired destruction and weakening of the muscular and elastic layers in the bronchial walls. Impaired clearance of secretions predisposes to recurrent infections, obstructions and further damage. Inflammation in surrounding alveoli can lead to peribronchial fibrosis 25,27.

##### Etiology

Commoner causes include infection, inflammation, congenital and proximal airway obstruction. Causes include 1-7,9,17,21:

- idiopathic, i.e. unknown (most common)

- impaired host defenses
  - [cystic fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cystic-fibrosis?lang=us) (most common cause in children)

  - [primary ciliary dyskinesia](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/primary-ciliary-dyskinesia?lang=us), e.g. [Kartagener syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/kartagener-syndrome-1?lang=us), [Young syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/young-syndrome-1?lang=us)

  - [primary immunodeficiency disorder](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/primary-immunodeficiency-diseases?lang=us), e.g. [common variable immunodeficiency](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/common-variable-immunodeficiency-pulmonary-manifestations-1?lang=us), [hypogammaglobulinaemia](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/hypogammaglobulinaemia?lang=us), [chronic granulomatous disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/chronic-granulomatous-disease-pulmonary-manifestations-2?lang=us)

  - [HIV/AIDS](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/hivaids?lang=us) 15,16
- postinfective (most common known non-cystic fibrosis cause in adults)
  - bacterial pneumonia and bronchitis, e.g. _S. aureus_, _H. influenzae_, _B. pertussis_

  - mycobacterial infection, e.g. [tuberculosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tb?lang=us), [Mycobacterium avium-intracellulare complex](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-mycobacterium-avium-complex-infection?lang=us)
- allergic and autoimmune
  - [allergic bronchopulmonary aspergillosis (ABPA)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/allergic-bronchopulmonary-aspergillosis?lang=us)

  - connective tissue disease, e.g. [rheumatoid arthritis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/rheumatoid-arthritis?lang=us) 6, [Sjögren disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/sjogren-disease-1?lang=us), [systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/systemic-lupus-erythematosus?lang=us) 7

  - [inflammatory bowel disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/inflammatory-bowel-disease?lang=us)
- obstruction
  - severe obstructive lung disease: [asthma](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/asthma-1?lang=us) or [chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD)](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/chronic-obstructive-pulmonary-disease-1?lang=us)

  - neoplasm, e.g. [bronchial carcinoid](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bronchial-carcinoid-tumour?lang=us), [lung cancer](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/lung-cancer-3?lang=us) inhaled foreign bodies
- congenital
  - bronchial tree malformations, e.g. [Mounier-Kuhn syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tracheobronchomegaly-1?lang=us), [Williams-Campbell syndrome](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/williams-campbell-syndrome?lang=us), [pulmonary sequestration](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-sequestration?lang=us), [bronchial atresia](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bronchial-atresia?lang=us)

  - [alpha-1-antitrypsin deficiency](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/alpha-1-antitrypsin-deficiency-4?lang=us)
- others
  - [chronic aspiration](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/chronic-aspiration-pneumonia?lang=us)

  - [traction bronchiectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/traction-bronchiectasis?lang=us) due to diffuse lung disease, e.g. [pulmonary fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/pulmonary-fibrosis?lang=us)

  - [radiation-induced lung disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/radiation-induced-lung-disease-1?lang=us)

  - post-transplantation

A mnemonic to remember common causes is [CAPT Kangaroo has Mounier-Kuhn](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/common-causes-of-bronchiectasis-mnemonic?lang=us).

##### Subtypes

According to macroscopic morphology, three types have been described which reflect chronicity and severity 8:

- [cylindrical bronchiectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cylindrical-bronchiectasis?lang=us)
  - bronchi have a uniform caliber, do not taper and have parallel walls ( [tram track sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tram-track-sign-chest-1?lang=us) and [signet ring sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/signet-ring-sign-bronchiectasis?lang=us))

  - commonest form 14
- [varicose bronchiectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/varicose-bronchiectasis?lang=us)
  - relatively uncommon

  - beaded appearances where dilated bronchi have interspersed sites of relative narrowing
- [cystic bronchiectasis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cystic-bronchiectasis?lang=us)
  - severe form with cyst-like bronchi that extend to the pleural surface

  - air-fluid levels are commonly present

According to one study, the relative prevalence were 14:

- cylindrical: ~45%

- varicose: ~10%

- cystic: ~45%

- multiple types: ~25%

#### Radiographic features

##### Plain radiograph

Chest x-rays are often abnormal but non-specific. Ring shadows and [tram-track opacities](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tram-track-sign-chest-1?lang=us) represent end-on and en-face dilated bronchi respectively. Air-fluid levels in cystic bronchiectasis may indicate current infection. [Peri-bronchovascular fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/missing?article%5Btitle%5D=peri-bronchovascular-fibrosis&lang=us) may obscure the bronchovascular structures 8,9.

##### CT

CT is diagnostic and may help identify the cause. A number of features are helpful in diagnosing bronchiectasis 8,9:

- bronchus visualized within 1 cm of the pleural surface 20
  - especially true of lung adjacent to costal pleura

  - most helpful sign for early cylindrical change
- lack of tapering

- increased [bronchoarterial ratio](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/broncho-arterial-ratio?lang=us) 9
  - diameter of a bronchus should measure approximately 0.65-1 times that of the adjacent pulmonary artery branch

  - between 1 and 1.5 may be seen in normal individuals, especially those living at high altitude

  - greater than 1.5 indicates bronchiectasis

A number of ancillary findings are also recognized:

- bronchial wall thickening: normally the wall of bronchus should be less than half the width of the accompanying pulmonary artery branch 25

- mucoid impaction 25

- [air-trapping](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/air-trapping?lang=us) and [mosaic perfusion](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/mosaic-perfusion?lang=us)

- [bronchial arterial enlargement](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bronchial-arterial-enlargement?lang=us)

Signs described on CT include:

- [tram-track sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/tram-track-sign-chest-1?lang=us)

- [signet ring sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/signet-ring-sign-bronchiectasis?lang=us)

- [string of pearls sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/string-of-pearls-sign-disambiguation?lang=us) 9

- [bunch of grapes sign](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bunch-of-grapes-sign-bronchiectasis?lang=us) 9

#### Treatment and prognosis

In general, the treatment of bronchiectasis is medical and involves promoting sputum clearance, using positional physiotherapy, and early and aggressive treatment of pulmonary infections. In some cases, chronic prophylactic administration of antibiotics (including inhaled antibiotics) may be required. In certain etiologies, specific pharmacotherapy may be available (e.g. cystic fibrosis transmembrane regulator (CFTR) modulators in [cystic fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cystic-fibrosis?lang=us)).

In cases where bronchiectasis is severe and localized, surgical resection of the affected lobe may be beneficial provided respiratory reserve is adequate. In cases where both lungs are extensively involved (e.g. [cystic fibrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/cystic-fibrosis?lang=us)), bilateral sequential lung transplantation can be considered 10.

Significant or recurrent hemoptysis may require bronchial artery embolization or surgery may be necessary 29-30.

#### History and etymology

The term bronchiectasis is derived from the Greek wordsbronckos (βρόγχος) meaning airway/loop and ectasis (έκταση) meaning widening.

#### Differential diagnosis

Reversible bronchial dilatation can occur, and is relatively common in organizing pneumonia.
