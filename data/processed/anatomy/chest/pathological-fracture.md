**Pathological fractures** are fractures that occur in abnormal bone and occur spontaneously or following minor trauma that would not otherwise fracture biomechanically normal bone.

#### Terminology

The term **pathological fracture** is usually reserved for [tumors](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/bone-tumors-overview?lang=us "Bone tumors (overview)"), either benign or malignant, although it has been used in the setting of [osteomyelitis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteomyelitis?lang=us). It can be used in the setting of generalized [metabolic bone disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/metabolic-bone-diseases?lang=us "Metabolic bone diseases") (e.g. [Paget disease](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/paget-disease-bone?lang=us), [osteopetrosis](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteopetrosis?lang=us)), although the term [insufficiency fracture](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/insufficiency-fracture?lang=us) is probably more correct 4. Insufficiency fractures are fractures due to multiple minor events causing a cumulative load on weakened [osteoporotic bone](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/osteoporosis-3?lang=us). [Fragility fractures](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/fragility-fracture?lang=us), on the other hand, are acute fractures in osteoporotic patients due to a single event of minimal trauma.

#### Pathology

##### Location

The most common locations for pathological fractures are 4:

- subtrochanteric [femur](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/femur?lang=us)

- humeral head and metaphyseal junction

- [vertebral body](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/vertebral-body-pathological-fracture?lang=us)

In addition, in adult patients, the [avulsion](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/avulsion-injury-1?lang=us "Avulsion fracture") of the femoral lesser trochanter should be considered a pathological fracture until proven otherwise 4.

#### Radiographic features

##### Radiography and CT

- destruction or focal defect within the inner layer of the bony cortex

- aggressive [periosteal reaction](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/periosteal-reaction?lang=us "Periosteal reaction")

- lytic lesion

- soft-tissue mass

- mineralized matrix 4

##### MRI

- absent or ill-defined fracture line

- well-defined, homogenous T1-hypointense abnormal signal without normal intervening marrow signal

- adjancent muscle edema 4

##### Scintigraphy

- diffuse uptake of radiopharmaceutical by the lesion 4

#### Treatment and prognosis

Pathological fractures are feared by oncologists because they may cause immobilization of their patients, especially when the spine or lower extremities are affected.

#### Practical points

A radiologist should mention the possibility of a pathological fracture if an osteolytic metastasis is seen. In principle, every osteolytic focus in the femoral neck or the spine is at risk of a pathological fracture.

Scoring systems have been developed to assess the fracture risk of bone metastases, the [Mirels classification](https://radiopaedia.org/articles/mirels-classification-pathological-fracture-risk-1?lang=us) is the one that has gained the most traction, although its poor sensitivity (35%) means that it is not without its controversy 5.
