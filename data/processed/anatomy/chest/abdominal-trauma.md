**Abdominal trauma** is usually divided into blunt and penetrating trauma.

#### Findings of abdominal trauma

- 
[h](/articles/haemoperitoneum?lang=us)[aemoperitoneum](/articles/haemoperitoneum?lang=us)

- 
[splenic trauma](/articles/splenic-trauma?lang=us): most common

- [hepatic trauma](/articles/liver-trauma?lang=us)

- [renal trauma](/articles/renal-trauma-1?lang=us)

- [pancreatic trauma](/articles/pancreatic-trauma-1?lang=us)

- 
[gastrointestinal tract (bowel) trauma](/articles/bowel-and-mesenteric-trauma?lang=us):

proximal jejunum is most commonly affected by blunt trauma, followed by the duodenum and ascending colon at the ileocecal valve region

- descending colon is only rarely involved

- less common abdominal trauma injuries:

gallbladder

- ureter

- stomach

- [adrenal](/articles/adrenal-gland-trauma?lang=us)

- [urinary bladder trauma](/articles/urinary-bladder-rupture?lang=us)

- vascular trauma: [abdominal aortic injury](/articles/abdominal-aortic-injury?lang=us) and other major abdominal and pelvic vessel injuries (e.g. inferior vena cava, renal vessels, celiac axis, superior mesenteric vessels, lumbar vessels, and iliac vessels)

- [abdominal wall trauma](/articles/rectus-sheath-haematoma?lang=us)

- [diaphragmatic rupture](/articles/diaphragmatic-rupture?lang=us)

- [retroperitoneal hemorrhage ](/articles/retroperitoneal-haemorrhage?lang=us)

- [hypoperfusion complex](/articles/ct-hypoperfusion-complex?lang=us)
